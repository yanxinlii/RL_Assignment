# Business Memo

## Retail Inventory Replenishment Agent Evaluation

The purpose of this project was to evaluate whether a reinforcement learning approach could improve inventory replenishment decisions in a retail environment. The idea was straightforward: instead of relying on a fixed inventory rule, train an agent to learn from experience and make ordering decisions that maximize long-term profitability. To test this idea, a Q-learning agent was developed and compared against a simple rule-based replenishment policy in a simulated retail environment.

After completing the training and evaluation process, my conclusion is that the current reinforcement learning agent should not be deployed in a real business setting. While the project successfully demonstrated the reinforcement learning workflow and produced a functioning learning agent, the final results showed that the baseline policy significantly outperformed the learned policy.

The most important finding from the evaluation was the difference in overall performance between the two approaches. The rule-based baseline achieved an average reward of approximately 6,906, while the Q-learning agent achieved an average reward of approximately 3,872. In addition, the baseline policy maintained healthy inventory levels throughout most episodes and experienced very few stockouts. By contrast, the Q-learning agent frequently allowed inventory levels to fall too low, resulting in a large number of stockout days and missed sales opportunities.

From a business perspective, this behavior would be difficult to accept. Inventory shortages directly affect revenue and customer satisfaction. If customers repeatedly encounter out-of-stock products, they may choose to purchase from competitors instead. Although the reinforcement learning agent was technically optimizing the reward function provided in the simulation, the resulting behavior would likely create operational challenges in a real retail environment.

One reason for this outcome is the simplicity of the state representation. The agent only observes a limited set of information before making a decision. While this was sufficient for implementing tabular Q-learning, it does not fully reflect the information that would normally be available to inventory managers. Real inventory decisions are often influenced by demand trends, seasonality, supplier reliability, promotional campaigns, holidays, and external market conditions. None of these factors were included in the current state space.

Another limitation is the simulation environment itself. The demand process in this project was intentionally simplified so that the focus could remain on reinforcement learning concepts rather than complex forecasting models. However, real-world demand is rarely random in the way it is represented in the simulator. Retail demand often follows patterns that evolve over time, and businesses usually have access to historical data that can provide useful signals about future demand. Because the environment does not capture these complexities, the learned policy may not transfer well to a real operating environment.

The project also highlights an important lesson about reinforcement learning in business applications. It is easy to assume that a learning algorithm will automatically outperform a simple rule-based system. However, the results of this project suggest that this assumption is often incorrect. In practice, the quality of the environment design, the reward function, and the state representation can be just as important as the learning algorithm itself. A poorly specified reinforcement learning problem can produce worse results than a straightforward business heuristic.

Despite the underwhelming performance of the Q-learning agent, I would not consider the project unsuccessful. In fact, the outcome provides useful insight into the challenges of applying reinforcement learning to operational decision-making problems. The experiment demonstrates that reinforcement learning requires careful problem formulation and extensive testing before deployment. Discovering that a simple baseline outperforms a learned policy is itself a valuable finding because it helps identify weaknesses in the current design.

If this project were being conducted within an organization, my recommendation would be to keep the model in a shadow-testing phase rather than deploying it directly. Under this approach, the reinforcement learning agent would continue generating replenishment recommendations, but inventory managers would remain responsible for making the final decisions. This would allow additional data collection and model refinement without exposing the business to unnecessary risk.

Looking forward, there are several improvements that could strengthen the model. The first would be expanding the state representation to include more information about demand history and inventory trends. A second improvement would be redesigning the reward function to better align with business objectives such as service levels and customer satisfaction. Finally, it would be useful to compare the current Q-learning implementation against more advanced reinforcement learning methods such as Deep Q-Networks (DQN) or Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO).

In conclusion, the current reinforcement learning agent is not ready for production use. The baseline policy achieved better overall performance and demonstrated greater operational stability throughout the evaluation period. Nevertheless, the project successfully illustrates the reinforcement learning process and highlights several important considerations when applying RL techniques to business problems. With a richer state representation, improved reward design, and a more realistic simulation environment, reinforcement learning may eventually become a viable approach for inventory management decisions.
